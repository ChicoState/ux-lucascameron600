For my journal entry, I chose to interact with Microsoft Clipchamp. To give some context, I am proficient in other, possibly more professional-grade, video editing software like Sony Vegas Pro. These other editing programs have much more capability, but also a much steeper learning curve. I am hoping that Microsoft Clipchamp will provide a very simple and familiar interface, possibly at a trade-off for more advanced features, as seen in other programs like Sony Vegas Pro.

Upon first loading the program, I am greeted with a very sleek (aesthetically pleasing) interface with a home menu and a few large buttons immediately available and highlighted. This was exactly what I was hoping this program would provide for me to start. I don’t need complicated options and settings—I just want something simple. This program is, so far, doing a good job at being learnable.

In my next step, I click on “create a new video.” At this point, it is pretty clear what I’d like to do next. There is a large box for me to import my media and instantly start working on it. The tabs on the side inform me that I can do other things with the media I’m currently dealing with, but they are not overwhelming.

<img width="982" height="665" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/74a15baa-37f5-44bb-9c41-e20e61044df4" />

I next import my media and start working with it. I quickly added different audio, muted the original video’s volume, and changed the aspect ratio. While this process did go rather quickly, I found that I was constantly frustrated with small bugs in how the program would run. It would cut everything correctly, but it would take a very long time to import new media, and watching the video preview was very choppy. This made it hard for me to edit the video in the exact way that I wanted, and it ended up being very unsatisfying. While it didn’t truly affect the amount of work I was able to perform, I just didn’t love the experience.

<img width="1895" height="1021" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/c784c528-c3e4-408f-886a-57f197953254" />

As mentioned, I was quickly able to make the simple edits I wanted, and then I continued to the process of exporting. Exporting was quick and efficient—it just asked what I wanted to name it and the export aspect ratio. Typically, in those other video editing programs, exporting can be an absolute nightmare of options because the software must support every range of use, making it extremely complicated. But not this one the menu was simple, sleek, and easy to understand.

<img width="857" height="475" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/898e3ad4-5ab7-44e3-ac19-f6a217c4a263" />

Overall, my experience using Microsoft Clipchamp was good. I think the design was simple, good-looking, and easy to use. I was able to quickly do what I wanted without much limitation, but also without an overwhelming amount of options. The interface was well designed, and I did not have to look around (system status/core functionality visible) much for what I wanted. If they were able to improve the functionality of the video preview and performance in rendering, the experience would become more satisfying, and I would want to use it even more. This program is very simple and usable, and while it is not perfect, it does a good job of making sure the user has a good experience.
